## SLIDE 7 — The Science

**Headline:**
This is not a theory. The science is clear.

**On-slide research blocks:**
- **CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey:** 42% of high school students report persistent sadness or hopelessness.
- **Harvard Business Publishing, 2024:** 4 of the 5 most in-demand workplace skills are human skills — self-awareness, resilience, flexibility, and agility.
- **Albert Bandura, Stanford University:** Self-efficacy — belief in your own ability — is a stronger predictor of success than actual ability.
- **Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence:** EQ predicts adult career success more reliably than IQ alone.

**Speaker notes:**
I know this audience. You are analytical. You want data before you decide.

So let me ground this in a few key findings.

The CDC data shows that emotional struggle is not a fringe issue. The Harvard Business Publishing finding shows that the world our children are entering values human skills deeply, even though schools rarely teach them directly. Bandura’s work on self-efficacy matters because what a child believes about their ability changes how they approach challenge. And Yale’s work reminds us that emotional intelligence is not separate from success. It helps shape it.

This is why I see this as foundational, not optional.

## SLIDE 8 — Why Early Matters

**Headline:**
Patterns that are not addressed early do not simply disappear

**On-slide evidence blocks:**
- **Childhood is the easier window:** early patterns become easier to repeat over time.
- **Stress affects cognition:** negative emotional states impair focus, memory, and decision-making.
- **Emotional regulation is learnable:** children can build better responses with practice.

**Bottom line:**
This is not crisis intervention. It is early strength-building.

**Speaker notes:**
When foundational patterns are ignored early, they usually show up later at a higher cost.

Emotional patterns work the same way. The anxious response, the shutdown, the self-doubt — if they are not addressed in childhood, they compound. They become the way this child responds to pressure at 16, at 25, and beyond.

That is why I care so much about timing. Childhood is the easier window for helping children recognize patterns, understand them, and build healthier responses before they harden.

## SLIDE 11 — The 6-Week Toolkit

**Headline:**
The 6-week program: the toolkit students build over time

**On-slide framing:**
Why this format matters:
- Spaced practice improves retention
- Weekly home practice improves transfer into real life
- Reflection between sessions helps children return with their own evidence

**Suggested table:**

| Session | Focus | Toolkit students build | How it gets reinforced |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Awareness | noticing thoughts and stress patterns | guided exercises + first home practice |
| 2 | Thought patterns | recognizing unhelpful thinking earlier | reflection + between-session observation |
| 3 | Emotions and body signals | noticing what stress feels like in the body | body-based tools + repetition |
| 4 | Constructive response | shifting thoughts and responses more deliberately | practice with real-life scenarios |
| 5 | Personal agency | taking ownership of the story they tell themselves | structured reflection + application |
| 6 | Integration | a fuller personal resilience toolkit and practice plan | review, consolidation, and long-term carryover |

**Bottom line:**
This is the fuller architecture of the work — and the toolkit students take with them.

**Speaker notes:**
For parents who want to know what the fuller program actually looks like, this is the clearest high-level view.

The 6-week format is not simply longer. It is developmentally stronger because students learn something, practice it at home, come back with real-life experience, and build on it. That weekly spacing matters. It improves retention, transfer, and ownership.

So by the end of the 6 weeks, students are not just exposed to ideas. They leave with a toolkit they have already begun using in real life.
