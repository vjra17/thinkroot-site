Slide 1
Building Children Who Can Handle Life
Subhead
An introduction to ThinkRoot Young Leaders and Resilience Lab

Tina Puri | Founder, ThinkRoot Young Leaders

Speaker Notes
Thank you for being here. I know most parents in this room are already doing a great deal for their children. You are investing in academics, activities, enrichment, and opportunity. My goal today is not to add pressure. It is to show you a missing layer that many children still need, even when they are doing well on paper. This presentation is about the skills that shape how children handle pressure, setbacks, emotions, responsibility, and real life. It is also an introduction to ThinkRoot Young Leaders and to the programs we are building, especially Resilience Lab.
Slide 2
Your child may be doing well in school. But what happens when life does not go according to plan?
On Slide
  • A disappointing result
  • A difficult friendship
  • A mistake they did not expect
  • A moment of pressure
  • A challenge they cannot solve right away
Speaker Notes
This is the real question I want to begin with. Many children are doing well in school. They are bright. They are capable. They are active. They may even look confident from the outside. But what happens when life does not go according to plan? What happens when they fail, when they compare themselves, when they feel overwhelmed, when they do not get the result they expected, or when they face a social or emotional challenge they cannot think their way out of? Because that is where a different set of abilities begins to matter.
Slide 3
What parents are already seeing
  • Panic before exams, even when prepared
  • Shutting down after one mistake
  • Avoiding challenge for fear of failure
  • Comparing constantly with others
  • Getting overwhelmed more quickly than expected
  • Confidence that disappears when things go wrong
These are not isolated moments. They point to a missing skill set.
Speaker Notes
Many parents are already seeing some version of this at home. The child who studies but goes blank under pressure. The child who gives up quickly after one setback. The child who is doing well externally, but is internally much less steady than they appear. These moments are easy to dismiss as part of growing up. But when they repeat, they usually point to something deeper: a child who has never been taught what to do in those moments.
Slide 4
This is not a personality problem. It is a skills gap.
  • Not lack of intelligence
  • Not weakness
  • Not a fixed personality trait
  • Not something children simply “grow out of”
It is a missing toolkit, and toolkits can be built.
Speaker Notes
This is one of the most important shifts in the entire presentation. When a child shuts down, spirals, avoids, or overreacts, we often interpret it as personality. We think they are sensitive, not confident, not mature enough, or simply not ready. But very often, the issue is not personality. The issue is that no one has taught them how to work with what happens when life gets hard. That is why I see this as a skills gap, not a character flaw.
Slide 5
The gap schools do not fill
What children are taught
  • Content
  • Performance
  • Competition
  • Achievement
  • Following systems
What children are rarely taught
  • How to recover after a mistake
  • How to stay steady under pressure
  • How to respond to frustration constructively
  • How to work with self-doubt
  • How to keep going when confidence drops
Speaker Notes
Schools teach many important things. This is not an argument against academics. But schools are not designed to teach children what to do when fear rises before an exam, when frustration takes over, when comparison affects confidence, or when a setback starts turning into a story about who they are. So children are often left to figure this out on their own. Some do. Many do not. And that affects far more than feelings. It affects performance, relationships, judgment, and how they meet life.
Slide 6
Why this matters so much now
  • Pressure starts early
  • Comparison is constant
  • External success can hide internal fragility
  • Low frustration tolerance shows up sooner
  • Children are expected to handle more without being shown how
The need is growing earlier, not later.
Speaker Notes
Parents today are raising children in a more competitive, more stimulating, and more comparison-driven environment than ever before. Pressure starts early. Children notice rankings, outcomes, and peer comparison much sooner. At the same time, many of them have less practice with frustration, uncertainty, and delayed gratification. So the need for these skills is not theoretical. It is immediate.
Slide 7
This is not a theory. The science is clear.
CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey42% of high school students report persistent sadness or hopelessness.
Harvard Business Publishing, 20244 of the 5 most in-demand workplace skills are human skills — self-awareness, resilience, flexibility, and agility.
Albert Bandura, Stanford UniversitySelf-efficacy — belief in your own ability — is a stronger predictor of success than actual ability.
Yale Center for Emotional IntelligenceEQ predicts adult career success more reliably than IQ alone.
Speaker Notes
I know this audience. You are analytical. You want data before you decide. So let me ground this in a few key findings. The CDC data shows that emotional struggle is not a fringe issue. The Harvard Business Publishing finding shows that the world our children are entering values human skills deeply, even though schools rarely teach them directly. Bandura’s work on self-efficacy matters because what a child believes about their ability changes how they approach challenge. And Yale’s work reminds us that emotional intelligence is not separate from success. It helps shape it. This is why I see this as foundational, not optional.
Slide 8
Patterns that are not addressed early do not simply disappear
Childhood is the easier windowEarly patterns become easier to repeat over time.
Stress affects cognitionNegative emotional states impair focus, memory, and decision-making.
Emotional regulation is learnableChildren can build better responses with practice.
This is not crisis intervention. It is early strength-building.
Speaker Notes
As engineers and builders, you understand technical debt. If foundational issues are ignored early, the cost shows up later. Emotional patterns work the same way. The anxious response, the shutdown, the self-doubt — if they are not addressed in childhood, they compound. They become the way this child responds to pressure at 16, at 25, and beyond. That is why I care so much about timing. Childhood is the easier window for helping children recognize patterns, understand them, and build healthier responses before they harden.
Slide 9
What makes ThinkRoot different
  • Not just behavior correction
  • Not just motivation
  • Not just “be positive”
  • A structured, practical approach to what drives behavior underneath
ThinkRoot goes deeper — to the root cause.
Speaker Notes
Many programs try to correct behavior at the surface. Some try to boost confidence through encouragement alone. Others offer tools, but without a deeper framework. ThinkRoot is different because it focuses on what is happening underneath behavior: the pattern, the interpretation, the emotional response, and the practiced habit that drives what the child does next. That is why I say ThinkRoot goes deeper, to the root cause.

Parents do not invest in a program because it sounds thoughtful. They invest because they want to see a difference in how their child handles life. The outcomes we care about are not vague. Slide 11
The 6-week program: the fuller developmental path
  • Session 1 — Meet Your Brain: learning to notice thoughts rather than be run by them
  • Session 2 — Thought Weather: recognizing storm thoughts and accepting that they pass
  • Session 3 — The Feeling Body: learning to read the body’s signals and respond with care
  • Session 4 — Kindness Is a Superpower: practicing healthier thought patterns and how they affect behavior
  • Session 5 — I Am the Author: building influence over the story a child tells themselves
  • Session 6 — I Am the Root: integrating the full model, self-compassion, and longer-term practice
This is the fuller architecture of the work.
Speaker Notes
For parents who want to know what the fuller program actually looks like, this is the high-level path. It is designed as a developmental sequence, not a collection of disconnected activities. The 6-week format gives children time to practice at home between sessions, return with real-life experience, and deepen the work week by week. That is what makes it the fuller pathway.
Slide 12
The 5-day Resilience Lab: a shorter, powerful entry point
  • Built from the same core philosophy
  • Covers the same core concepts as Sessions 1–5
  • More compact and introductory
  • A strong first step for families who want to begin now
The 5-day format is the entry point. The 6-week format is the deeper build.
Speaker Notes
The 5-day Resilience Lab is not a separate idea. It is the shorter entry point into the same work. It gives children a practical introduction to the core concepts and tools in a compact format. For some families, that is exactly the right place to begin. For others, the longer six-week format may be the better fit because it allows more reinforcement, reflection, and between-session practice.
Slide 13
What students actually do in the program
  • Guided reflection
  • Discussion
  • Practical tools
  • Structured exercises
  • Journaling and drawing
  • Home practice and carryover
This is active skill-building, not passive listening.
Speaker Notes
Parents often ask whether this is just talking, or whether children are actually doing something concrete. The program is active. Children reflect, discuss, practice tools, work through examples, and begin applying what they learn to everyday situations. The goal is not just understanding. The goal is use. In the 6-week format especially, each session includes home practice review and a new home practice assignment, because real life between sessions is part of the learning.
Slide 14
What parents actually notice
  • A longer pause before a meltdown
  • Children naming feelings instead of acting them out
  • Using breathing or a calming tool independently
  • Greater empathy and patience toward siblings and classmates
  • Better sleep and less mental overactivity at night
  • More ownership of thoughts, reactions, and choices
Speaker Notes
These are the kinds of changes parents tend to notice first. Not abstract transformation. Observable shifts. Often it looks like a child pausing a little longer before reacting. Naming what they are feeling more clearly. Using a tool on their own. Recovering faster. Sleeping better. Showing more empathy. Taking more ownership. That is important, because this work should not stay theoretical. Parents should be able to notice it in daily life.
