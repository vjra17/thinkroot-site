## SLIDE 4 — Reframe

**Headline:**
This is not a personality problem. It is a skills gap.

**On-slide contrast:**
- Not lack of intelligence
- Not weakness
- Not a fixed personality trait
- Not something children simply “grow out of”

**Standout line:**
It is a missing toolkit, and toolkits can be built.

**Speaker notes:**
This is one of the most important shifts in the entire presentation.

When a child shuts down, spirals, avoids, or overreacts, we often interpret it as personality. We think they are sensitive, not confident, not mature enough, or simply not ready.

But very often, the issue is not personality. The issue is that no one has taught them how to work with what happens when life gets hard.

That is why I see this as a skills gap, not a character flaw.

## SLIDE 5 — The Missing Gap

**Headline:**
The gap schools do not fill

**Left column:**
What children are taught
- Content
- Performance
- Competition
- Achievement
- Following systems

**Right column:**
What children are rarely taught
- How to recover after a mistake
- How to stay steady under pressure
- How to respond to frustration constructively
- How to work with self-doubt
- How to keep going when confidence drops

**Speaker notes:**
Schools teach many important things. This is not an argument against academics.

But schools are not designed to teach children what to do when fear rises before an exam, when frustration takes over, when comparison affects confidence, or when a setback starts turning into a story about who they are.

So children are often left to figure this out on their own. Some do. Many do not. And that affects far more than feelings. It affects performance, relationships, judgment, and how they meet life.

## SLIDE 6 — Why This Matters Now

Why this matters so much now

**On-slide points:**
- Pressure starts early
- Comparison is constant

- Low frustration tolerance shows up sooner
- Children are expected to handle more without being shown how

**Bottom line:**

**Speaker notes:**

So the need for these skills is not theoretical. It is immediate.

## SLIDE 7 — The Science

**Headline:**
This is not a theory. The science is clear.

**On-slide research blocks:**
- **CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey:** 42% of high school students report persistent sadness or hopelessness.
- **Harvard Business Publishing, 2024:** 4 of the 5 most in-demand workplace skills are human skills — self-awareness, resilience, flexibility, and agility.
- **Albert Bandura, Stanford University:** Self-efficacy — belief in your own ability — is a stronger predictor of success than actual ability.
- **Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence:** EQ predicts adult career success more reliably than IQ alone.

**Speaker notes:**
I know this audience. You are analytical. You want data before you decide.

So let me ground this in a few key findings.

The CDC data shows that emotional struggle is not a fringe issue. The Harvard Business Publishing finding shows that the world our children are entering values human skills deeply, even though schools rarely teach them directly. Bandura’s work on self-efficacy matters because what a child believes about their ability changes how they approach challenge. And Yale’s work reminds us that emotional intelligence is not separate from success. It helps shape it.

This is why I see this as foundational, not optional.

---

## SLIDE 8 — Why Early Matters

**Headline:**
Patterns that are not addressed early do not simply disappear

**On-slide evidence blocks:**
- **Childhood is the easier window:** early patterns become easier to repeat over time.
- **Stress affects cognition:** negative emotional states impair focus, memory, and decision-making.
- **Emotional regulation is learnable:** children can build better responses with practice.

**Bottom line:**
This is not crisis intervention. It is early strength-building.

**Speaker notes:**
As engineers and builders, you understand technical debt. If foundational issues are ignored early, the cost shows up later.

Emotional patterns work the same way. The anxious response, the shutdown, the self-doubt — if they are not addressed in childhood, they compound. They become the way this child responds to pressure at 16, at 25, and beyond.

That is why I care so much about timing. Childhood is the easier window for helping children recognize patterns, understand them, and build healthier responses before they harden.

## SLIDE 11 — The Full Pathway

**Headline:**
The 6-week program: the fuller developmental path

**On-slide session overview:**
- **Session 1 — Meet Your Brain:** learning to notice thoughts rather than be run by them
- **Session 2 — Thought Weather:** recognizing storm thoughts and accepting that they pass
- **Session 3 — The Feeling Body:** learning to read the body’s signals and respond with care
- **Session 4 — Kindness Is a Superpower:** practicing healthier thought patterns and how they affect behavior
- **Session 5 — I Am the Author:** building influence over the story a child tells themselves
- **Session 6 — I Am the Root:** integrating the full model, self-compassion, and longer-term practice

**Bottom line:**
This is the fuller architecture of the work.

**Speaker notes:**
For parents who want to know what the fuller program actually looks like, this is the high-level path.

It is designed as a developmental sequence, not a collection of disconnected activities.

The 6-week format gives children time to practice at home between sessions, return with real-life experience, and deepen the work week by week. That is what makes it the fuller pathway.
