What Parents Can Expect

One program. Two pacing options.

Both formats teach the same core skills. The 5-Day Intensive gives students an immersive start and daily repetition. The 6-Week Series gives them time to practice between sessions, return with real-life examples, and build stronger long-term retention.

Designed to give children practical tools they can use now — and skills they can carry for life.
Overview

What Resilience Lab helps students build

Resilience Lab is designed to help students handle the pressures that quietly shape school, sports, friendships, and daily life. Instead of giving them more advice, it gives them a clear process and repeated practice so the skills become usable when they actually need them.

✓Recognizing unhelpful thoughts earlier
Students learn how to catch limiting thoughts sooner and know what to do next instead of being run by them.
✓Confidence, discipline, and calmer responses
They begin responding to pressure, mistakes, and frustration with more steadiness and stronger self-trust.
✓Recovery after setbacks
They build the ability to recover faster from self-doubt, disappointment, conflict, and everyday pressure.
✓A repeatable technique they can use on their own
The work is designed to be used independently at school, at home, before competition, after setbacks, and in everyday life.
Formats

Two ways to experience Resilience Lab

Both formats are being offered as summer options. The content is aligned across them. The difference is pace, repetition, and how much time students have to practice between sessions and apply the work in real life.

Format One
5-Day Intensive

A focused one-week experience for students who benefit from immersion, repetition, and momentum.

What To Expect

What happens in a session

Resilience Lab stays practical. Sessions are designed to be memorable, usable, and age-appropriate rather than theoretical, so children leave knowing what to do in real situations.

🧠
Learn the big idea
Students are introduced to one clear concept about how attention, self-talk, stress, and behavior affect performance and daily life.
🔁
Practice the skill
They learn through guided exercises, repeated practice, reflection, and simple age-appropriate activities that make the work stick.
🚀
Apply it in real life
Students begin using the skills in school, sports, family life, friendships, and moments of real pressure, frustration, or self-doubt.
A Closer Look

How the learning unfolds

The 5-day and 6-week versions share the same core arc. The difference is how quickly students move through it and how much real-life practice they accumulate between sessions.

5-Day learning arc
Students move through a compact sequence: learning how the mind works, recognizing different thought patterns, noticing how stress shows up in the body, practicing regulation and self-compassion, and integrating everything into a personal toolkit by Day 5.
6-Week learning arc
Sessions 1–5 cover the same core concepts with more time to practice between meetings. Session 6 deepens integration and helps students build a longer-term plan they can continue using on their own.
What students take home
Depending on format, students leave with a Personal Resilience Toolkit, daily or weekly practice cards, and a repeatable framework they can use any time, anywhere.
Why parents choose the weekly format
Spaced practice supports stronger long-term retention. The weekly model lets children test the work in everyday life and come back with six weeks of their own experience.

Reported Outcomes

The kinds of changes students reported

These are examples of the kinds of shifts students reported after practicing this work: stronger academic confidence, calmer responses during exams, better self-regulation after conflict, and more independent use of the tools in daily life.

Academic confidence
One student began approaching math differently, improved her grades, and eventually started helping classmates who were struggling in the same way.
Calm under pressure
Students reported using the practice during exams to relax, steady themselves, and perform with more composure.
Better habits and self-control
A student significantly reduced daily screen time on his own, and as that changed, academic performance, social confidence, and steadiness improved.
