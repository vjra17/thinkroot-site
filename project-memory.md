## Audience

Primary audience:
- busy, highly technical Bay Area parents
- many are software professionals
- they tend to prioritize academics and sports over wellness
- they have limited time and will defer if the site feels dense or theoretical

## Core Homepage Strategy

- Sell the program first and explain the depth second.
- Lead with practical outcomes under pressure, not philosophy or methodology.
- Keep the visual design system; improve messaging hierarchy and conversion clarity.
- Position the site as a high-intent interest-capture page, not a direct buy-now enrollment page.
- Present AAA and Five Power as proof that a real method exists, but do not teach them in full early on the homepage.
- Make ThinkRoot feel practical, credible, efficient, and differentiated.

## Homepage Framework

Use this section flow for the homepage:

1. Hero
- Goal: make a busy cold parent feel immediate relevance and keep reading.
- Job: name the missing skill, connect to outcomes parents already care about, and establish a credible modern tone.

2. Gap
- Goal: make the parent recognize the problem.
- Job: show what children are not being taught and why this matters in real life.

3. Why It Matters
- Goal: raise the stakes.
- Job: show how the missing skill layer affects academics, sports, confidence, relationships, and performance.

4. Why ThinkRoot
- Goal: show differentiation.
- Job: make clear this is not generic SEL, vague wellness, or surface-level coping.

5. How It Works
- Goal: reduce ambiguity.
- Job: show there is a real, usable method without dumping full curriculum detail.

6. Program Options
- Goal: show fit.
- Job: help parents understand what is offered now and which format may suit their child.

7. Real Stories / Proof
- Goal: build trust.
- Job: make outcomes believable through lived examples.

8. Founder Credibility
- Goal: answer why parents should trust Tina.
- Job: show practical experience and scientific seriousness without creating a second main storyline.

9. Community / Future Pathway
- Goal: show the larger vision without distracting from the main conversion.
- Job: signal that the work can grow into leadership and contribution, but keep this secondary to Resilience Lab.

10. Interest List CTA
- Goal: convert.
- Job: make the next step feel easy, credible, and low-risk for a cold audience.

## Vocabulary Rule

- Do not repeat the same headline vocabulary across the homepage.
- Each section should advance the same core strategy using different language.
- Avoid overusing the same anchor words in consecutive sections, including:
  - `pressure`
  - `setbacks`
  - `self-doubt`
  - `inner`
  - `root`
  - `skills schools do not teach`
- Repeat the strategic idea, not the exact wording.
- Keep the page scientific and credible, but do not make parents study the theory before they value the program.

## Key Positioning Decisions

- ThinkRoot should feel like a force multiplier for academics, sports, confidence, and resilience, not a competitor to them.
- The site should emphasize that children are rarely taught how to manage pressure, setbacks, self-talk, and inner reactivity.
- `Self-intelligence` is a core brand idea.
- Messaging should remain secular, practical, and research-grounded.
- Avoid drifting into language that reads as vague, spiritual, or overly therapeutic for this audience.

## Launch Model

Current launch model:
- interest capture / priority interest list

This means:
- parents are being asked to express serious interest
- not to enroll and pay immediately
- groups may later be formed based on age, area, and demand

Preferred CTA direction:
- `Join the Priority Interest List`
- or comparable early-access / priority-access language
